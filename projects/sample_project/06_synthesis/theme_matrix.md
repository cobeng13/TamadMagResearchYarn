## Theme 1: Pre-board / mock examination performance as predictor of licensure success

### Summary

Multiple Philippine Radiologic Technology (RT) and related radiography studies report that institutional mock/pre-board exam scores are positively associated with licensure or certification outcomes. Effect sizes vary (moderate correlations, significant logistic regression odds ratios, and strong discriminant-function coefficients in multisite institutional analyses). However, allied-health evidence shows mixed results where some pre-board instruments predicted licensure (radiography HESI, institutional mock exams) while other single-site allied-health pre-board measures did not (one dentistry study). Alignment of pre-board content and psychometric quality appears important and is repeatedly noted as a potential modifier of predictive validity.

### Direct evidence

- Rabinoa2025MockBoardExaminationResult: Mock board exam grade correlated with RTLE pass/fail (point-biserial/Pearson r reported -0.415 interpreted as higher mock → higher pass probability) and logistic regression showed B = 0.195, Exp(B) = 1.215 (odds increase ≈21.5% per one-point increase in mock score).
- Ampaso2022: Institutional periodic (monthly) exam scores strongly correlated with institutional revalidation exam scores (r = 0.904, p < 0.001) and were a significant predictor in regression (unstandardized β = 1.010, model R² = 0.818) — suggesting internal periodic assessments can strongly predict later institutional comprehensive exam performance (revalidation).
- Garcia2026: In a large multi-program Philippine institutional sample (n = 933 across programs), mock/pre-board exam performance was one of the strongest discriminators in a discriminant function for PRC pass/fail classification (unstandardized coefficient reported .053; large classification accuracy reported).

### Indirect/background evidence

- ChristineGouveia2024PredictiveValidityHesiRadiography (HESI Radiography Exit Exam, US): HESI exit exam scores were strongly associated with ARRT first-time pass rates across 25 U.S. programs; multiple operationalizations of HESI were significant predictors and program policies (minimum score, required prep) had indirect effects via HESI performance.
- Flores2024 (Dentistry, Laguna): GWA correlated with licensure score, but pre-board comprehensive exam did not correlate with licensure outcome (Pearson r = -0.037, p = 0.798) in that single-site dentistry sample — an example of a non-predictive pre-board in allied health.

### Mixed or conflicting findings

- Flores2024 found no significant relationship between pre-board comprehensive exam results and licensure outcome (dentistry).
- Ampaso2022 found that case-study scores (another form of internal assessment) did not predict revalidation exam scores, despite strong prediction from monthly exams — indicating not all internal assessments are equally predictive.

### Methodological notes

- Pre-board predictive strength depends on instrument alignment with licensure content, timing relative to licensure, and psychometric properties (several sources flag alignment/validation as critical).
- Analytic approaches reported include point-biserial/ Pearson correlations, logistic regression, discriminant function analysis (DFA), and cross-validation (DFA study reported classification accuracy and cross-validated accuracy).
- Correction-for-guessing and item-level cognitive classification (lower vs higher-order) are methodological considerations for MCQ-based pre-board tests (Bera2024 methodological guidance).

### Limitations/cautions

- Some pre-board studies are single-site or review-center based and may not generalize across institutions; pre-board instruments may differ substantially across contexts.
- Instrument psychometric validation is often absent or limited in the sources; unvalidated pre-board scores may over- or under-estimate predictive validity.
- Some studies excluded failers or retakers, potentially biasing predictive estimates (Flores2024 excluded non-passers/retakers).

## Theme 2: Clustered academic performance and professional-course grade groups as predictors

### Summary

Evidence from both RT-specific and allied-health studies supports overall academic performance (GWA/GPA) and professional-course grade groups as meaningful predictors of licensure/certification outcomes. For the current study, this evidence supports the cautious use of clustered academic grades as the primary academic predictor set. Meta-analysis in PT shows strong correlations for in-program GPAs; dentistry and radiography studies likewise show positive associations between GWA/professional course averages and licensure scores. Professional-course averages may perform as strong discriminators in multivariable models.

### Direct evidence

- Oliva2021 (BSRT): institutional GWA used as academic outcome (study-habit predictors to GWA); demonstrates operationalization of GWA in RT programs though study-habit predictors were not significant.
- Cabatingan2024 (Admission test study): Institutional reporting found weak relationship between admission test stanine and average professional licensure rating — suggests admission indicators may be weaker than in-program academic indicators (details To be confirmed).

### Indirect/background evidence

- Flores2024 (Dentistry): GWA significantly positively correlated with licensure exam score (Pearson r = 0.531, p = 0.000).
- Wolden2019 (Systematic review, PT): Meta-analytic results show first- and later-year GPAs have large relationships with licensure exam outcomes (first-year PTGPA Mρ ≈ 0.52; third-year PTGPA Mρ ≈ 0.51); comprehensive exams had medium effects (Mρ ≈ 0.49); clinical performance had weak effects.
- Garcia2026 (multi-program DFA): Professional knowledge (mean professional course grades) had the largest unstandardized coefficient (.074) and pronounced mean differences between passers and non-passers, indicating professional-course averages can be key discriminators.

### Mixed or conflicting findings

- Some small RT studies (e.g., Oliva2021) show small explained variance for behavioral predictors of GWA and highlight that small single-site samples may lack power to detect associations.
- Admission-test measures (Cabatingan2024) may show limited predictive value compared with in-program GPA/professional grades.

### Methodological notes

- Operationalization of GWA/professional-course averages varies across institutions; for this study, course-to-cluster mapping, cluster weighting, and scale conversion should be confirmed before comparing or modeling clustered academic grades.
- Meta-analytic evidence supports prioritizing in-program GPA as a predictor but underscores heterogeneity across studies and disciplines.

### Limitations/cautions

- Disciplinary and jurisdictional differences (PT literature is US-focused; dentistry RT contexts differ) limit direct generalization — local validation in Philippine RT is needed.
- GWA may be affected by differing grading scales and course composition across institutions (To be confirmed).

## Theme 3: Clinical / internship / practicum performance

### Summary

RT and allied-health studies show mixed or limited predictive value for clinical/practicum performance when predicting multiple-choice licensure outcomes. In some large multi-program analyses practicum ratings contributed but with limited discriminatory power, often due to restricted range or high mean ratings. PT meta-analysis found clinical performance weakly correlated with licensure exam scores.

### Direct evidence

- Garcia2026: Internship/practicum performance had a positive coefficient (.044) in DFA but small mean differences between passers and non-passers (both 'Distinction' level) suggesting limited discriminatory power possibly due to ceiling effects.
- Ampaso2022: Institutional case-study scores (a practical/problem-based assessment) did not predict revalidation exam scores (r = 0.127, p = 0.537), while monthly written exams did.

### Indirect/background evidence

- Wolden2019 systematic review: Clinical performance scores showed small and non-significant relationships with licensure performance (first clinical Mρ = 0.10; final clinical Mρ = 0.13).

### Mixed or conflicting findings

- Some institutions report high practicum means for both passers and non-passers (Garcia2026), limiting discrimination; other contexts may show different distributions — evidence is heterogeneous.

### Methodological notes

- Practicum instruments vary in rubric, rater training, and scale; restricted range and rater leniency/ceiling effects can reduce predictive utility.
- Ensure distributional checks for clinical ratings before including them as continuous predictors.

### Limitations/cautions

- High mean practicum ratings and limited variability may produce weak associations despite practical relevance; consider alternative scoring or domain-level breakdowns.
- Clinical ratings may capture different competencies (observed performance) than written licensure exams (knowledge/multiple-choice), so moderate associations are expected.

## Theme 4: Non-cognitive, attitudinal, and readiness measures

### Summary

Non-cognitive and attitudinal measures have mixed evidence for predicting licensure-related outcomes. In RT-specific small studies, grit did not predict institutional revalidation (Ampaso2022), while attitudinal/readiness self-assessments are correlated with perceived preparedness but are not linked to objective licensure data in the reported RT study.

### Direct evidence

- Ampaso2022: Grit (Grit-S) had no significant correlation with revalidation exam scores (r = -0.043, p = 0.833) and was not a significant predictor in regression.
- Gallardo2025: Attitude and preparedness composites correlated moderately (r = .562, p = .000) within RT graduates but the study reported no objective licensure or academic outcomes—so attitudinal measures are descriptive only in that study.

### Indirect/background evidence

- Wolden2019: Meta-analysis found small but significant combined effect for noncognitive measures (Mρ ≈ 0.26) predicting NPTE in PT programs, suggesting some non-cognitive predictors can add incremental validity in multivariable models.

### Mixed or conflicting findings

- Grit predicted performance in some prior studies noted by authors but not in Ampaso2022's small RT sample — indicating possible sample-size or context-dependent effects.

### Methodological notes

- Non-cognitive scales require validity evidence (criterion-related) against objective outcomes; small samples can obscure moderate effects.
- Self-report measures capture perceptions that may not map directly to objective exam performance.

### Limitations/cautions

- Do not rely solely on self-reported readiness/attitude as proxies for objective licensure preparedness without concurrent validity evidence.
- Grit or similar constructs may require larger samples to detect small-to-moderate effects.

## Theme 5: Assessment design, psychometrics, and measurement issues

### Summary

Methodological evidence emphasizes rigorous assessment construction and psychometric evaluation of pre-board/internal exams before using them as predictors. Items should be mapped to a blueprint, cognitive levels documented, difficulty ratings collected, correction-for-guessing considered for MCQs, and instrument reliability/reproducibility assessed (e.g., Cohen's Kappa for agreement). AI-response studies illustrate these methods but are methodological reference rather than evidence of human predictive relationships.

### Direct evidence

- Bera2024: Demonstrated construction of image-rich board-style items aligned to a blueprint, use of Bloom taxonomy, difficulty ratings, correction-for-guessing adjustments, and reproducibility assessment (Cohen's Kappa).

### Indirect/background evidence

- ChristineGouveia2024PredictiveValidityHesiRadiography: Examined multiple operationalizations and cutoffs of a standardized exit exam (HESI), sensitivity analyses, and program-policy mediation—illustrates best practices for operationalizing pre-board scores.

### Mixed or conflicting findings

### Methodological notes

- Psychometric validation (item analysis, reliability, blueprint alignment) is essential; many institutional pre-board exams lack reported psychometric evidence.
- Measurement choices (raw score vs corrected score; cognitive-level mix) can change predictive relationships—sensitivity analyses recommended.

### Limitations/cautions

- Using unvalidated pre-board instruments risks misestimating predictive validity and making incorrect remediation decisions.
- Correction-for-guessing and other adjustments should be explicitly reported to aid interpretability and replication.

## Theme 6: Institutional and cohort/contextual predictors (aggregate-level effects)

### Summary

Institutional-level analyses show cohort size and institutional passing rate can predict counts of failed examinees in Poisson/negative binomial models. Aggregated models are useful for institutional planning but do not substitute for individual-level predictors (GWA, pre-board scores) when predicting individual licensure outcomes.

### Direct evidence

- Ibañez2025: Institution-level retrospective longitudinal models (Poisson/negative binomial) across five RT schools found total number of examinees and institutional passing rate associated with number of failed examinees in some institutions; other predictors were inconsistent across institutions.
- Garcia2026: Contextual variables (study duration, count of repeated courses) discriminated pass vs fail in DFA and are examples of student-level contextual variables that can be included alongside academic predictors.

### Indirect/background evidence

- To be confirmed.

### Mixed or conflicting findings

- Ibañez2025 reported that in several institution-specific models none of the included aggregated predictors reached significance — indicating heterogeneity across institutions and limitations of aggregated predictors.

### Methodological notes

- Aggregated count models require attention to overdispersion and appropriate model choice (Poisson vs negative binomial) as performed in Ibañez2025.
- Aggregated results can mask individual-level variance; multi-level modeling or inclusion of contextual covariates is advisable when combining institutional and individual predictors.

### Limitations/cautions

- Findings at institution-year aggregation level cannot be used to infer individual-level predictive relationships (ecological inference risk).
- Missing data for some years (e.g., licensure disruptions due to COVID-19) complicates longitudinal institutional modeling.

## Theme 7: Program policies, remediation, and review practices

### Summary

Program policies (e.g., setting minimum exit/pre-board scores, requiring preparatory activities, and remediation) are associated with higher pre-board and licensure pass rates in radiography and multi-program Philippine studies, often operating indirectly by improving pre-board scores. Local RT studies propose programmatic interventions based on mock/pre-board findings but evaluations of these interventions are limited.

### Direct evidence

- Rabinoa2025MockBoardExaminationResult: Proposed enhancement program (weekly mock/comprehensive tests, peer mentoring, intensified review phases) based on mock→licensure association, but interventions not empirically evaluated.
- Ibañez2025 and Ampaso2022 recommended strengthening institutional supports and alignment of monthly exams with target competencies.

### Indirect/background evidence

- ChristineGouveia2024PredictiveValidityHesiRadiography: Program policies (minimum HESI score, required test preparation) had indirect effects on ARRT pass rates mediated via HESI performance; programs implementing best-practice policies had higher pass rates.

### Limitations/cautions
