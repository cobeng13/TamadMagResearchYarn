# Research Brief

## Working Title
Predictive Validity of Academic Performance, Course-Cluster Grades, and Pre-Board Examination Results for Radiologic Technologist Licensure Examination Performance

## Brief Study Description
This study will examine whether undergraduate academic performance, course-cluster grades, and pre-board examination results are associated with and predict Radiologic Technologist Licensure Examination outcomes among Bachelor of Science in Radiologic Technology graduates in the Philippines. The study will use a retrospective descriptive-correlational and predictive design based on available academic records, pre-board examination results, and licensure examination outcomes. The study should be framed as an institution-level predictive validation study, not as a broad causal study of what determines RTLE success.

## Research Problem
Radiologic Technology graduates must pass the Radiologic Technologist Licensure Examination to become registered practitioners. However, licensure performance varies across graduates and institutions. Programs need evidence-based indicators that can identify students who may need academic support before taking the licensure examination. Academic grades, course-cluster performance, internship or clinical performance if available, terminal competency assessments if available, and pre-board examinations may help predict licensure success, but their relative and incremental predictive value in the local institutional context remains a narrow and researchable issue requiring further review and analysis.

## Context and Rationale
The study is situated in Philippine Radiologic Technology education and licensure preparation. Prior literature in health professions education suggests that academic performance, professional course grades, clinical performance, admission indicators, comprehensive examinations, and mock board examinations may be associated with licensure outcomes. Radiologic Technology-specific literature should be prioritized. Allied health studies in Nursing, Medical Technology, Dentistry, Pharmacy, and related programs should be used only as supporting evidence where Radiologic Technology literature is limited.

## Study Design
Retrospective descriptive-correlational and predictive research design.

## Population
Graduates of the Bachelor of Science in Radiologic Technology program who have taken the Radiologic Technologist Licensure Examination.

## Setting
Philippines. Specific institution, academic years, and licensure examination periods: To be confirmed.

## Data Sources
- Academic records
- Professional course grades
- Course-cluster grades or domain-specific course averages, if available
- Internship or clinical performance grades, if available
- Pre-board examination results
- Terminal competency or comprehensive examination results, if available
- Radiologic Technologist Licensure Examination outcomes
- Demographic or profile variables, if available

## Intended Analysis
- Descriptive statistics for academic, pre-board, and licensure profiles
- Correlation analysis between predictor variables and licensure ratings or subject-area scores
- Multiple linear regression or comparable modeling for RTLE rating if a continuous board rating is available
- Logistic regression or comparable classification modeling for pass/fail status if binary outcome coding is available
- Incremental predictive-value analysis to determine whether pre-board results add explanatory value beyond academic and course-cluster grades, if sample size and data quality allow
- Additional subgroup or subject-area analysis: To be confirmed

## Scope Boundary
The study should focus on institution-level predictive validity of academic, course-cluster, and pre-board indicators of RTLE performance among Radiologic Technology graduates. It should not attempt to evaluate the entire curriculum, compare institutions broadly, evaluate teaching effectiveness, or infer causality unless the study design and data support such claims. Practical recommendations should be framed as screening, forecasting, early warning, and remediation-planning uses, not as guarantees of passing.
